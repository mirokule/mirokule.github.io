DEFINITIONS

Converges, Diverges, Limit

The sequence ${a_n}$ converges to the number $L$ if to every positive number $\epsilon$ there corresponds an integer $N$ such that for all $n$,

$$ n > N \;\;\;\; \Rightarrow \;\;\;\; \left|a_n-L\right| < \epsilon. $$

L is limit of the sequence.

If no such number $L$ exists, we say that ${a_n}$ diverges.

Deverges to Infinity

The sequence ${a_n}$ diverges to infinity if for every number $M$ there is an integer $N$ such that for all $n$ larger than $N$, $a_n > M$. If this condition holds we write

$$ \lim_{n\rightarrow ∞} a_n = ∞ \;\;\;\; or \;\;\;\; a_n\rightarrow ∞. $$

Nondecreasing Sequence

A sequence ${a_n}$ with the property that $a_n \leq a_{n+1}$ for all $n$ is called a nondecreasing sequence.

Power Series

A power series about $x=0$ is a series of the form
$$ \sum_{n=0}^{∞}c_nx^n = c_0+c_1x+c_2x^2+\cdot \cdot \cdot +c_nx^n+\cdot \cdot \cdot $$
A power series about $x=a$ is a series of the form
$$ \sum_{n=0}^{∞}c_n(x-a)^n = c_0+c_1(x-a)+c_2(x-a)^2+\cdot \cdot \cdot +c_n(x-a)^n+\cdot \cdot \cdot $$

Tayloer Series

The Taylor series generated by $f$ at $x=a$ is
$$ \sum_{k=0}^{∞} \frac{f^{(k)(a)}}{k!}(x-a)^k $$

THEOREM

The Sandwich Theroem for Sequences

If $a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n$ holds for all $n$ beyond some index $N$, and if $\lim_{n\rightarrow ∞}a_n=\lim_{n\rightarrow ∞}c_n=L$, then $\lim_{n\rightarrow ∞}b_n = L$ also.

The Continuous Function Theorem for Sequences

Let ${a_n}$ be a sequence of real numbers. If $a_n\rightarrow L$ and if $f$ is a function that is continuous at $L$ and defined at all $a_n$, then $f(a_n)\rightarrow f(L)$.

The Comparison Test

Let $\Sigma a_n$ be a series with no negative terms.

(a) $\Sigma a_n$ converges if there is a convergent series $\Sigma c_n$ with $a_n \leq c_n$ for all $n>N$, for some integer N.

(b) $\Sigma a_n$ diverges if there is a divergent series of nonnegative terms $\Sigma d_n$ with $a_n \geq d_n$ for all $n>N$, for some integer N.

The Ratio Test

Let $\Sigma a_n$ be series with positive terms and suppose that

$$ \lim_{n\rightarrow ∞} \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \rho $$

Then
(a) the series converges if $\rho < 1$,
(b) the series diverges if $\rho > 1$ or $\rho$ is infinite,
(c) the test is inconclusive if $\rho = 1$.

The Root Test

Let $\Sigma a_n$ be a series with $a_n \geq 0$ for $n\geq N$, and suppose that

$$ \lim_{n\rightarrow ∞} \sqrt[n]{a_n} = \rho $$

Then
(a) the series converges if $\rho < 1$,
(b) the series diverges if $\rho > 1$ or $\rho$ is infinite,
(c) the test is inconclusive if $\rho = 1$.

The Alternating Series Test(Leibniz’s Theorem)

The series

$$ \sum_{n=1}^{∞}(-1)^{n+1} u_n = u_1 - u_2 + u_3 - u_4 + … $$

converges if all three of the following conditions are satisfied:
(1) The $u_n$’s are all positive.
(2) $u_n \geq u_{n+1}$ for all $n\geq N$, for some integer $N$.
(3) $u_n \rightarrow 0$.

The sum L of the series with an error whose absolute value is less than $u_{n+1}$, the remainder, $L-s_n$, has the same sign as the first unused term.

The Convergence Theorem for Power Series

If the power series $\sum_{n=0}^{∞}a_nx^n = a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\cdot \cdot \cdot$ converges for $x=c\neq 0$, then it converges absolutely for all $x$ with $\left|x\right|<\left|c\right|$.If the series diverges for $x=d$, then it diverges for all $x$ with $\left|x\right|>\left|d\right|$

The Term-by-Term Differentiation Theorem

If $\sigma c_n(x-a)^n$ converges for $a-R0$, it defines a function $f$:
$$ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{∞}c_n(x-a)^n, \;\;\;\;\;\; a-R<x<a+R $$
Such a function $f$ has derivatives of all orders inside the interval of convergence.

The Term-by-Term Integration Theorem

Suppose that
$$ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{∞}c_n(x-a)^n $$
converges for $a-R0)$. Then
$$ \sum_{n=0}^{∞}c_n\frac{(x-a)^{n+1}}{n+1} $$
converges for $a-R<x<a+R$ and
$$ \int f(x)dx = \sum_{n=0}^{∞}c_n\frac{(x-a)^{n+1}}{n+1} $$
for $a-R<x<a+R$.

The Remainder Estimation Theorem

If there is a positive constant $M$ such that $\left|f^{(n+1)}(t)\right|\leq M$ for all $t$ between $x$ and $a$, then the remainder term $R_n(x)$ satisfies the inequality
$$ \left|R_n(x)\right| \leq M\frac{\left|x-a\right|^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} $$
